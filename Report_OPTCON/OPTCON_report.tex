\begin{justify}
    
    This project investigates optimal control strategies for a quadrotor equipped with a suspended load, a system characterized by nonlinear and highly coupled dynamics. The study focuses on three methods: Newton’s method for trajectory generation, Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) for trajectory tracking, and Model Predictive Control (MPC) for predictive constrained control.
    
    Newton’s method is applied to compute optimal state-input trajectories between equilibrium points, exploiting second-order information for fast convergence. Both step-based and smooth reference trajectories are evaluated, with results showing that smooth references improve convergence speed and accuracy. Based on these trajectories, the system is linearized and an LQR controller is designed to provide robust tracking and disturbance rejection. Finally, an MPC framework is developed to handle system and input constraints while ensuring smooth control actions. Simulations demonstrate that MPC achieves superior tracking in the presence of disturbances, although with higher computational cost.
    
    The comparative analysis highlights the complementary nature of these approaches: Newton’s method is effective for trajectory optimization, LQR ensures reliable real-time performance, and MPC provides flexibility under constraints. These findings contribute to the development of advanced control solutions relevant to applications in aerial robotics and autonomous systems.

\end{justify}

\section{Nonlinear Dynamic Modeling of the Quadrotor System}
\label{sec:Nonlinear_Dynamics}

The quadrotor with suspended load is modeled as per given constrains of the project. 
The suspended pendulum introduces additional dynamic couplings between translational and rotational states, which make the system nonlinear and underactuated.  

The continuous-time dynamics of the system are given by:
\begin{align}
\dot{x}_p &= v_x, \\
\dot{y}_p &= v_y, \\
\dot{\alpha} &= \omega_\alpha, \\
\dot{\theta} &= \omega_\theta, \\
\dot{v}_x &= \frac{1}{(M+m)}mL \omega_\alpha^2 \sin(\alpha) \nonumber\\
 &-  F_s \left(\sin(\theta) - \frac{m}{M}\sin(\alpha-\theta)\cos(\alpha)\right), \\
\dot{v}_y &= \frac{1}{(M+m)}(-mL \omega_\alpha^2 \cos(\alpha) \nonumber\\
 &+ F_s \left(\cos(\theta) + \frac{m}{M}\sin(\alpha-\theta)\sin(\alpha)\right) - (M+m)g, \\
\dot{\omega}_\alpha &= \frac{-F_s \sin(\alpha - \theta)}{M L}, \\
\dot{\omega}_\theta &= \frac{\ell F_d}{J}.
\end{align}

\subsection*{Interpretation of the Model}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Translational dynamics:} The horizontal acceleration $\dot{v}_x$ is influenced not only by the roll angle $\theta$ but also by the pendulum’s oscillations. 
    The vertical acceleration $\dot{v}_y$ balances gravitational force, pendulum swing, and the total thrust $F_s$.  

    \item \textbf{Pendulum dynamics:} The suspended mass swings due to the difference in motion between the quadrotor body and the load. 
    The term $-F_s \sin(\alpha - \theta)$ couples the quadrotor roll and the pendulum angular motion.  

    \item \textbf{Rotational dynamics:} The angular acceleration of the quadrotor, $\dot{\omega}_\theta$, is directly proportional to the differential thrust $F_d$, 
    scaled by the lever arm $\ell$.  
\end{itemize}


This nonlinear model serves as the foundation for trajectory generation and controller design. While the full nonlinear dynamics are used for simulation and analysis, 
many control strategies (e.g., LQR, MPC) require linearized approximations around an equilibrium point or reference trajectory. 
This motivates the next step: discretization and linearization.

\section{Discretization of the System Dynamics}
\label{sec:Discretization}

To implement control algorithms in a digital computation environment, 
the continuous-time nonlinear dynamics must be discretized. 
The system is expressed in compact form as
\begin{equation}
\dot{x}(t) = f(x(t), u(t)),
\end{equation}
where $x \in \mathbb{R}^8$ is the state vector and $u \in \mathbb{R}^2$ is the control input.  

For numerical implementation, the forward Euler method is adopted:
\begin{equation}
x_{k+1} = x_k + \Delta t \, f(x_k, u_k),
\end{equation}
where $\Delta t$ is the discretization step size.  

Expanding the equations yields the discrete-time model:
\begin{align}
x_p^{(k+1)} &= x_p^{(k)} + \Delta t \, v_x^{(k)}, \\
y_p^{(k+1)} &= y_p^{(k)} + \Delta t \, v_y^{(k)}, \\
\alpha^{(k+1)} &= \alpha^{(k)} + \Delta t \, \omega_\alpha^{(k)}, \\
\theta^{(k+1)} &= \theta^{(k)} + \Delta t \, \omega_\theta^{(k)}, \\
v_x^{(k+1)} &= v_x^{(k)} + \Delta t \, \Phi_x(x^{(k)},u^{(k)}), \\
v_y^{(k+1)} &= v_y^{(k)} + \Delta t \, \Phi_y(x^{(k)},u^{(k)}), \\
\omega_\alpha^{(k+1)} &= \omega_\alpha^{(k)} + \Delta t \, \Phi_\alpha(x^{(k)},u^{(k)}), \\
\omega_\theta^{(k+1)} &= \omega_\theta^{(k)} + \Delta t \, \Phi_\theta(u^{(k)}).
\end{align}



Here the nonlinear terms are defined as:

\begin{align}
\Phi_x(x,u) &= \frac{1}{M+m}\Big[mL \omega_\alpha^2 \sin(\alpha) \nonumber\\
&- F_s\Big(\sin(\theta) - \tfrac{m}{M}\sin(\alpha-\theta)\cos(\alpha)\Big)\Big], \\
\Phi_y(x,u) &= \frac{1}{M+m}\Big[-mL \omega_\alpha^2 \cos(\alpha) \nonumber\\
&+ F_s\Big(\cos(\theta) + \tfrac{m}{M}\sin(\alpha-\theta)\sin(\alpha)\Big) - (M+m)g\Big], \\
\Phi_\alpha(x,u) &= \frac{-F_s \sin(\alpha-\theta)}{ML}, \\
\Phi_\theta(u) &= \frac{\ell F_d}{J}.
\end{align}


\section{Linearization and Jacobian Computation}
\label{sec:Jacobian}

Many control techniques such as LQR and MPC require a linear model of the system. 
Since the quadrotor with suspended load is governed by nonlinear dynamics, 
it must be linearized around an operating point or a reference trajectory.  

The nonlinear discrete dynamics can be written as
\begin{equation}
x_{k+1} = f_d(x_k, u_k),
\end{equation}
where $f_d(\cdot)$ represents the discretized update function.  

To obtain a local linear approximation, we expand $f_d$ using a first-order Taylor series 
around the point $(x^\ast,u^\ast)$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta x_{k+1} = A \, \Delta x_k + B \, \Delta u_k,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
\Delta x_k = x_k - x^\ast, 
\qquad 
\Delta u_k = u_k - u^\ast.
\end{equation}

The system matrices $A$ and $B$ are the Jacobians of the discrete dynamics:
\begin{equation}
A = \frac{\partial f_d}{\partial x}\Big|_{(x^\ast,u^\ast)}, 
\qquad 
B = \frac{\partial f_d}{\partial u}\Big|_{(x^\ast,u^\ast)}. \\
\\
\end{equation}

\section{Fundamentals of Optimal Control Theory}

Optimal control theory is a mathematical framework concerned with determining a control policy for a dynamical system such that a prescribed performance objective is optimized. In the context of quadrotor systems, this objective typically involves maneuvering the vehicle along a desired trajectory while minimizing energy expenditure, ensuring stability, and respecting system dynamics.  

The finite-horizon nonlinear optimal control problem is formulated as:
\begin{equation}
\min_{
    \begin{matrix}
        x_1,\dots,x_T, \\ u_0,\dots,u_{T-1}
    \end{matrix}}
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1} 
\left( \tfrac{1}{2} \|x_t - x_{des,t}\|_{Q_t}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2} \|u_t - u_{des,t}\|_{R_t}^2 \right)
+ \tfrac{1}{2} \|x_T - x_{des,T}\|_{P_f}^2
\end{equation}
subject to
\begin{align}
    x_{t+1} &= f(x_t, u_t), \qquad t = 0,\dots,T-1, \\
    x_0 &= x_{\text{init}}.    
\end{align}

Here, $Q_t$, $R_t$ and $P_f$ are user-defined weighting matrices penalizing state deviations, control effort, and terminal error, respectively.
The sequences $x_{des,t}$ and $u_{des,t}$ represent desired reference trajectories that need not necessarily satisfy the dynamics exactly.

In the present work, the nonlinear dynamics $f(\cdot)$ of the quadrotor with suspended load (see Section \ref{sec:Nonlinear_Dynamics}) are used in this formulation. Discretization (Section \ref{sec:Discretization}) allows implementation in a digital simulation environment, while linearization (Section \ref{sec:Jacobian}) provides locally valid models that enable the application of Newton’s method, Linear Quadratic Regulation (LQR), and Model Predictive Control (MPC).

This framework bridges system modeling with algorithmic design, laying the theoretical foundation for the trajectory generation and control strategies developed in the subsequent chapters.

\chapter{Newton's Method for Optimal Control}

\section{Problem Formulation and Reference Trajectory}

The first step in applying Newton’s method for optimal control is the formulation of the reference trajectory. In the context of the quadrotor with suspended load, this trajectory represents a desired evolution of the system states that connects two feasible equilibria of the dynamics. The goal of the optimization procedure is not only to generate an admissible trajectory that satisfies the nonlinear dynamics, but also to minimize the associated quadratic cost function with respect to deviations from the reference.

\subsection{Equilibrium Point Computation}
Equilibrium points of the quadrotor–pendulum system are defined as states in which both the translational and rotational accelerations vanish under constant control inputs. Formally, an equilibrium $(x_e, u_e)$ satisfies:

\begin{equation}
    f(x_e, u_e) = 0,
\end{equation}

where $f(\cdot)$ represents the continuous-time system dynamics. For the planar quadrotor, this requires balancing gravitational forces with the supporting thrust $F_s$, while ensuring that the differential thrust $F_d$ is zero to avoid roll acceleration. Typical choices include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A hovering equilibrium at $(x_p, y_p) = (0,0) $ with $F_s = (M + m)g$, $F_d = 0$.
    \item A shifted equilibrium at $(x_p, y_p) = (0,0) $ with the same force balance, corresponding to the target state.
\end{itemize}

The determination of such equilibria can be automated by root-finding routines applied to the system equations. These equilibria serve as the initial and terminal conditions for the optimal control problem.

\subsection{Definition of the Reference Curve}

Once the equilibrium points are known, a reference trajectory must be defined between them. The reference is not necessarily dynamically feasible but provides a geometric guideline for the optimizer. For Task 1, the reference is modeled as a \textbf{step function} in position: the quadrotor is assumed to remain at the initial equilibrium for a prescribed duration, transition towards the target, and then stabilize at the final equilibrium.

This formulation is consistent with classical optimal control setups, where the desired path simplifies the design of cost matrices while leaving the optimizer to enforce feasibility through the system dynamics.

\subsection{Force Decomposition}

The control inputs of the system are expressed as:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Supporting force:} $F_s = F_l + F_r$, the total vertical thrust. At equilibrium, this exactly cancels the gravitational pull (M + m)g. Adjustments in $F_s$ enable altitude changes
    \item \textbf{Differential force:} $F_d = F_r - F_l$, responsible for roll torque and lateral displacement. Setting $F_d = 0$ eliminates roll dynamics, corresponding to a purely vertical maneuver.
\end{itemize}

In defining the reference curve, we consider the nominal case $F_d = 0$, $F_s=(M+m)g$, and all velocities and angular states equal to zero. This baseline reference provides the optimizer with a simple target to refine, while allowing Newton’s method to generate dynamically consistent trajectories that respect the nonlinear quadrotor–pendulum model.


\section{Implementation of Newton's Method}

Newton’s method is applied to solve the optimal control problem by iteratively refining the state–input trajectory in order to minimize the cost functional. The algorithm uses local linearizations of the dynamics and second-order approximations of the cost to compute descent directions that progressively improve feasibility and optimality.

\subsection{Algorithm Overview}
The procedure starts with an initial guess $(x^0, u^0)$ and updates it iteratively until convergence. At each iteration, gradients and Hessians of the dynamics and cost are computed, leading to a quadratic subproblem whose solution provides the update direction.

\subsection{Descent Direction Calculation}
The gradients and Hessians are computed as follows:
\begin{align}
&\text{Evaluate } \nabla_1 f_t(x_k^t, u_k^t), \nabla_2 f_t(x_k^t, u_k^t), \nabla_1 \ell_t(x_k^t, u_k^t),\nabla_2 \ell_t(x_k^t, u_k^t), \nabla \ell_T(x_k^T).\nonumber\\
&\text{Co-state Equation: } \lambda_k^T = \nabla \ell_T(x_k^T).\nonumber\\
&\text{Descent Direction: Solve the minimization problem:}\nonumber\\
&\min_{\Delta x, \Delta u} \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \left[ \begin{array}{c}
\nabla_1 \ell_t(x_k^t, u_k^t) \\
\nabla_2 \ell_t(x_k^t, u_k^t)
\end{array} \right]^{\top} \left[ \begin{array}{c}
\Delta x_t\\
\Delta u_t
\end{array} \right] + \frac{1}{2} \left[ \begin{array}{c}
\Delta x_t \\
\Delta u_t
\end{array} \right]^{\top} \left[ \begin{array}{cc}
Q_k^t & S_k^{t\top} \\
S_k^t & R_k^t
\end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c}
\Delta x_t \\
\Delta u_t
\end{array} \right] \nonumber\\
&\qquad {}+ \nabla \ell_T(x_k^T)^{\top}\Delta x_T + \frac{1}{2} \Delta x_T^{\top} Q_k^T \Delta x_T \\
&\text{s.t. } \Delta x_{t+1} = \nabla_1 f_t(x_k^t, u_k^t)^{\top}\Delta x_t + \nabla_2 f_t(x_k^t, u_k^t)^{\top}\Delta u_t, \Delta x_0 = 0 \nonumber
\end{align}


\subsection{Input trajectory Update}

Compute new state-input trajectory (implementing step-size selection rule, e.g., Armijo):

\begin{align}
OpenLoop &: u_{k+1}^t = u_k^t + \gamma_k \Delta u_k^t. \\
ClosedLoop &: u_{k+1}^t = u_k^t + u_k^t(x_{k+1}^t -x_k^t) + \gamma_k \sigma_t^K.   
\end{align}


\subsection{State Trajectory Update}
The state trajectory is updated by forward integrating with \( x_{k+1}^0 = x_{\text{init}} \) and \( x_{k+1}^{t+1} = f(x_{k+1}^t, u_{k+1}^t) \).

\subsection{Regularization Techniques}
To handle non-positive definite matrices, the following regularization is applied:
\begin{align*}
\tilde{Q}_k^t &:= \nabla_{2,11} \ell_t(x_k^t, u_k^t), \\
\tilde{R}_k^t &:= \nabla_{2,22} \ell_t(x_k^t, u_k^t), \\
\tilde{S}_k^t &:= \nabla_{2,12} \ell_t(x_k^t, u_k^t), \\
\tilde{Q}_k^T &:= \nabla_{2,11} \ell_T(x_k^T).
\end{align*}

\subsection{Affine LQR Problem}
The affine LQR problem is solved with the following optimal solution:
\begin{align}
    \Delta u_t^* &= K_t^* \Delta x_t^* + \sigma_t^*, \\
    \Delta x_{t+1}^* &= A_t \Delta x_t^* + B_t \Delta u_t^*, \\
    &\quad \text{for } t = 0, \ldots, T-1, \nonumber
\end{align}
where
\begin{align*}
    K_t^* &= -(R_t + B_t^{\top} P_{t+1} B_t)^{-1}(S_t + B_t^{\top} P_{t+1} A_t), \\
    \sigma_t^* &= -(R_t + B_t^{\top} P_{t+1} B_t)^{-1}\left( r_t + B_t^{\top} p_{t+1} + B_t^{\top} P_{t+1} c_t \right), \\
    p_t &= q_t + A_t^{\top} p_{t+1} + A_t^{\top} P_{t+1} c_t + K_t^{* \top} \left( R_t + B_t^{\top} P_{t+1} B_t \right) \sigma_t^*, \\
    P_t &= Q_t + A_t^{\top} P_{t+1} A_t - K_t^{* \top} (R_t + B_t^{\top} P_{t+1} B_t) K_t^*,
\end{align*}
with \( p_T = q_T \) and \( P_T = Q_T \).


\section{Explicit Computation of $\nabla J(u)$}
To compute $\nabla J(u)$ explicitly, consider the Lagrangian $L(x, u, \lambda)$ defined as:
\begin{equation}
    L(x, u, \lambda) = \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \ell_t(x_t, u_t) + \ell_T(x_T) + \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \left( f(x_t, u_t) - x_{t+1} \right)^{\top} \lambda_{t+1}.
\end{equation}
Given a trajectory $(x, u)$, such that $x_{t+1} = f_t(x_t, u_t)$ for $t = 0, 1, \ldots, T-1$ and a given $x_0$, the gradient at each time step $t$ is computed as follows:
\begin{align*}
    \lambda_t &= A_t^{\top} \lambda_{t+1} + a_t, \quad \text{for } t = T-1, \ldots, 1, \text{ with } \lambda_T = \nabla \ell_T(x_T), \\
    (\nabla J(u))_t &= B_t^{\top} \lambda_{t+1} + b_t, \quad \text{for } t = T-1, \ldots, 0,
\end{align*}
where we denote:
\begin{align*}
    A_t^{\top} &= \nabla_1 f(x_t, u_t), \\
    B_t^{\top} &= \nabla_2 f(x_t, u_t), \\
    a_t &= \nabla_1 \ell_t(x_t, u_t), \\
    b_t &= \nabla_2 \ell_t(x_t, u_t).
\end{align*}

\chapter{Trajectory Tracking via LQR}
\label{chap:Smooth_Trajectory}

This chapter addresses the design and implementation of a Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) for the trajectory tracking problem. The goal is to ensure that the quadrotor with a suspended load follows the optimal trajectory derived in Chapter \ref{chap:NewtonsMethod} while exhibiting robustness against disturbances. The LQR approach is particularly suitable due to its balance between computational efficiency and strong theoretical guarantees in stabilizing linearized dynamical systems.

\section{LQR-Based Trajectory Tracking}

The Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) is a feedback control method that minimizes a quadratic cost function over a discrete-time horizon. Its primary objective is to regulate deviations of the system’s state from the optimal trajectory while simultaneously penalizing excessive control effort. The resulting controller ensures a trade-off between trajectory accuracy and input energy efficiency.

\subsection{System Lineraization}
The quadrotor dynamics are nonlinear, and therefore a linear approximation is required around the optimal trajectory $(x_{opt}, u_{opt})$ The linearized system is expressed as:

\begin{equation}
\Delta x_{t+1} = A_{opt,t}\Delta x_t + B_{opt,t}\Delta u_t   
\label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}


where $\Delta x_t = x_t - x_{opt,t}$ and $\Delta u_t = u_t - u_{opt,t}$. The matrices $A_{opt,t}$ and $B_{opt,t}$ are obtained from the Jacobians of the system dynamics evaluated along the optimal trajectory. This linearization enables the application of quadratic optimization techniques inherent in LQR design.

\subsection{LQR Controller Design}

The Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) framework formulates the trajectory tracking problem 
as the minimization of a quadratic cost function subject to the linearized system dynamics. 
The optimization problem is given by

\begin{equation}
\min_{\Delta u} \; 
\sum_{t=0}^{T-1} 
\left( \Delta x_t^\top Q_{reg} \Delta x_t + \Delta u_t^\top R_{reg} \Delta u_t \right) 
+ \Delta x_T^\top Q_{reg,T} \Delta x_T,
\label{eq:lqr_cost}
\end{equation}

subject to the system dynamics in eq. \ref{eq:dynamics}

where $Q_{reg}$, $R_{reg}$, and $Q_{reg,T}$ are the state, input, and terminal cost weighting matrices, respectively.  

The solution of this problem is obtained via the backward Riccati recursion. 
The symmetric positive definite matrix $P_t$ and the optimal gain matrix $K_t$ are computed iteratively as follows:

\begin{align}
P_t &= Q_{reg,t} + A_{opt,t}^\top P_{t+1} A_{opt,t} \nonumber \\
&- \left(A_{opt,t}^\top P_{t+1} B_{opt,t}\right)
\left(R_{reg,t} + B_{opt,t}^\top P_{t+1} B_{opt,t}\right)^{-1}
\left(B_{opt,t}^\top P_{t+1} A_{opt,t}\right)
\end{align}

\begin{equation}
K_t = -\left(R_{reg,t} + B_{opt,t}^\top P_{t+1} B_{opt,t}\right)^{-1}
\left(B_{opt,t}^\top P_{t+1} A_{opt,t}\right).
\label{eq:gain}
\end{equation}

The recursion is initialized with $P_T = Q_{reg,T}$, 
and the process is repeated for all $t = T-1, \ldots, 0$. 
The resulting time-varying gain matrices $K_t$ define the optimal feedback law, 
ensuring regulation of the system states around the optimal trajectory.

\subsection{Disturbance Rejection and Trajectory Tracking}

The closed-loop control law is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
u_t = u_{opt,t} + K_t(x_t - x_opt,t)   
\end{equation}

which actively corrects deviations from the desired trajectory. The LQR controller thus guarantees asymptotic stability of the linearized system and ensures robustness to external disturbances or modeling uncertainties. Disturbance rejection arises from the feedback term, which continuously drives the state back towards the optimal reference trajectory.

\section{MPC Algorithm: Dynamic Optimization in a Prediction Horizon}

The essence of Model Predictive Control (MPC) lies in its iterative, predictive, and optimizing nature, particularly evident in the use of a finite prediction horizon. In the following, we present the core algorithmic steps of MPC in the context of quadrotor trajectory tracking.

\subsection{Predictive Control in a Rolling Horizon Framework}

MPC is based on the principle of repeatedly solving an optimization problem over a moving (receding) horizon. At each sampling time $t$, the controller predicts the evolution of the quadrotor states within a finite prediction horizon $T_{\text{pred}}$. This prediction accounts for both the system dynamics and possible external disturbances. By exploiting these predictions, MPC determines a control sequence that minimizes a prescribed cost function while respecting constraints.  

\subsection{Optimization at Each Step}

At each time step $t$, MPC solves the following optimization problem:  

\begin{equation}
\min_{x_t,\ldots,x_{t+T}, \; u_t,\ldots,u_{t+T-1}} 
\sum_{\tau=t}^{t+T-1} 
\Big( x_\tau^\top Q x_\tau + u_\tau^\top R u_\tau \Big) 
+ x_{t+T}^\top P x_{t+T}
\label{eq:mpc_cost}
\end{equation}

subject to the constraints:
\begin{align}
x_{\tau+1} &= f(x_\tau, u_\tau), \quad \tau = t, \ldots, t+T-1, \nonumber\\
x_t &= x_{\text{meas},t}, \nonumber\\
u_\tau &\in \mathcal{U}, \quad \tau = t, \ldots, t+T-1. \nonumber
\end{align}

Here, $x_t,\ldots,x_{t+T}$ and $u_t,\ldots,u_{t+T-1}$ denote the predicted state and input trajectories, respectively. The weighting matrices $Q$ and $R$ penalize deviations from the reference state and excessive control effort, while $P$ is the terminal state weighting matrix obtained from the Algebraic Riccati Equation (ARE). The function $f(\cdot)$ represents the nonlinear quadrotor dynamics, and $x_{\text{meas},t}$ is the measured system state at time $t$. The set $\mathcal{U}$ denotes the feasible input region, which incorporates actuator limitations.  

\subsection{Constraints}

In practice, quadrotor operation must satisfy physical and safety limitations. These are expressed as input and state constraints of the form:  
\begin{align}
u_{\min} \leq u_k \leq u_{\max}, \\
x_{\min} \leq x_k \leq x_{\max}.
\end{align}
These conditions ensure that the control inputs remain within the actuator bounds and that the states evolve within admissible operating regions.  

\subsection{Application of the Control Input and Receding Horizon Strategy}

Once the optimization problem is solved, only the first control input of the optimal sequence is applied to the quadrotor. At the next sampling instant, the state is remeasured, the horizon shifts forward by one step, and a new optimization problem is solved. This receding horizon strategy enables MPC to continuously adapt to changes in the quadrotor’s dynamics, disturbances, or modeling errors.  

\subsection{Iterative Nature and Computational Considerations}

The strength of MPC lies in its predictive capability and iterative structure, which combine foresight with adaptability. However, this advantage comes with the requirement of solving an optimization problem at each time step. For systems with fast dynamics, such as quadrotors, this results in significant computational demands. Efficient solvers and real-time implementation strategies are therefore essential for practical deployment of MPC.  


\section{MPC Algorithm Steps}
The MPC control loop at each sampling instant can be summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Measure the current state.  
    \item Solve the finite-horizon optimization problem.  
    \item Apply the first control input from the computed sequence.  
    \item Update the state prediction with the new system state.  
    \item Repeat at the next time step.  
\end{enumerate}

MPC has proven to be a robust strategy for quadrotor trajectory tracking, particularly under the influence of disturbances. Its ability to handle multivariable dynamics and enforce constraints in real time makes it a powerful tool for aerial robotics.  

\section{Conclusion}
This chapter demonstrated the application of Model Predictive Control to a quadrotor system. \section{Future Research Directions}

The analysis presented in this work highlights several promising directions for further research in the field of quadrotor optimal control. These extensions would enhance the robustness, efficiency, and applicability of the proposed methodologies:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Integration of Learning-Based Methods:}  
    The incorporation of machine learning techniques, such as reinforcement learning, into the MPC and LQR frameworks could improve adaptability to uncertain or unstructured environments. Hybrid schemes that combine data-driven models with physics-based dynamics may increase robustness to modeling inaccuracies.  

    \item \textbf{Energy Efficiency Optimization:}  
    While the present work focuses on trajectory tracking and stability, future research may address the optimization of energy consumption. Minimizing energy usage is particularly relevant for battery-powered aerial vehicles and could be achieved by explicitly including energy-related costs in the control formulation.  

    \item \textbf{Robustness under Environmental Variability:}  
    A natural extension of this study is to assess controller performance under diverse environmental conditions, such as wind disturbances or varying payloads. Robust MPC formulations or stochastic optimization approaches may be required to guarantee reliable operation.  

    \item \textbf{Scalability to Multi-Agent Systems:}  
    Extending the proposed control strategies to multi-quadrotor systems would enable cooperative behaviors such as formation flying and payload transport. The design of distributed MPC or decentralized LQR schemes represents an important challenge in scalability and coordination.  

    \item \textbf{Experimental Validation:}  
    Real-world testing remains a critical step for bridging the gap between theoretical analysis and practical deployment. Hardware-in-the-loop simulations and experimental prototypes would allow assessment of computational feasibility, sensor noise resilience, and actuator limitations.  
\end{enumerate}

In summary, these directions emphasize the importance of advancing control strategies beyond simulation-based studies. By integrating learning-based methods, energy-aware formulations, robustness analysis, scalability, and real-world validation, future work can significantly enhance the reliability and applicability of optimal control in aerial robotics.

\chapter*{Conclusions}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Conclusions} 

This report has presented a comprehensive study optimal control techniques applied to a quadrotor with a suspended load. The work began with the formulation of the system dynamics and applied Newton’s method for trajectory generation. The method proved effective in computing optimal transitions between equilibrium points, with smoother reference curves leading to faster convergence and reduced cost.

The Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) was then implemented for trajectory tracking. Simulation results confirmed its ability to stabilize the system and compensate for disturbances with minimal computational effort, demonstrating its suitability for real-time applications.

Finally, Model Predictive Control (MPC) was investigated as a predictive and constraint-aware strategy. MPC showcased strong performance in handling disturbances and respecting system constraints, offering flexibility and robustness, though at the cost of higher computational requirements.

Overall, the combination of these methods highlights the trade-offs between efficiency, robustness, and computational demand. Newton’s method is well-suited for open-loop trajectory design, LQR provides reliable feedback control, and MPC offers predictive capabilities for complex and constrained scenarios. Together, they establish a versatile framework for addressing the challenges of quadrotor control and provide a foundation for future research and practical deployment in aerial robotics.
